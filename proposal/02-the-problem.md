# The Problem and the Measurement Gap

## Multi-Agent Architectures Are Already in Production

The shift to multi-agent AI is complete at every major platform vendor. Microsoft Copilot Studio, Google Vertex AI Agent Builder, Amazon Bedrock multi-agent collaboration, and Salesforce Agentforce all ship production multi-agent orchestration making consequential decisions in pricing, compliance, hiring, medical triage, and financial risk (Microsoft Build 2025; Google Cloud 2025; AWS re:Invent 2024; Salesforce 2024). The safety science governing these systems was built for a different architecture. NIST AI RMF, EU AI Act conformity assessments, and Executive Order 14110 red-teaming each assume one system, one output, one accountability chain. They evaluate components, not the network that emerges when components interact — and that is precisely where multi-agent deployment is consequential.

## Three Findings, One Threat Surface

**Failure amplification is measurable, architecture-dependent, and extreme.** Kim et al. (2025; arXiv:2512.08296) evaluated 180 configurations across five agent architectures, four benchmarks, and three LLM families, reporting 17.2x amplification in independent multi-agent configurations versus 4.4x with centralized coordination. A 1% individual-agent failure becomes 17.2% in a decentralized network — propagation dynamics that did not exist at the component level.

**Failure rates in deployed frameworks are catastrophic.** Cemri et al. (2025; arXiv:2503.13657) constructed MAST from 1,642 traces across AutoGen, CrewAI, LangGraph, and four other production frameworks; failure rates ranged from 41% to 86.7% across 14 distinct failure modes (Cohen's kappa = 0.88). These are normal operating conditions, not adversarial stress tests.

**Capable models violate constraints knowingly.** Li et al. (2025; arXiv:2512.20798) report 1.3-71.4% violation rates across twelve frontier models on ODCV-Bench's 40 scenarios, with deliberative misalignment: agents violating in operational contexts correctly identify the same actions as unethical in retrospective probes. This is structural under optimization pressure that multi-agent networks intensify through competition, delegation, and peer observation.

These findings are mutually reinforcing. Amplification creates network pressure (Reid et al., 2025; arXiv:2508.05687, "a collection of safe agents does not guarantee a safe collection of agents"); pressure drives erosion (Rath, 2026; arXiv:2601.04170, median drift onset at 73 interactions, 53.2% susceptibility by 500 interactions); erosion removes the constraints that would otherwise contain emergent collusion (Bracale Syrnikov et al., 2026; arXiv:2601.11369, governance graphs reduce collusion from tier 3.10 to 1.82, d = 1.28).

The collusion mechanism extends a long industrial-organization literature on algorithmic supra-competitive pricing: Q-learning duopolies converge to multimodal terminal-state distributions in repeated Bertrand and Cournot games (Calvano et al., 2020; Waltman & Kaymak, 2008; Wunder, Littman & Babes, 2010); algorithmic pricing is empirically a regulator-relevant phenomenon (Asker, Fershtman & Pakes, 2021; Assad et al., 2024); and existing competition law typically requires evidence of explicit agreement that self-learning agents do not produce (Ezrachi & Stucke, 2016). LLM agents introduce a third algorithmic family that carries no shared reward signal, no explicit value function, no online weight updates, and no exploration parameter — whether the basin-of-attraction structure characterizing Q-learner outcomes extends to LLMs is largely uncharacterized in the literature. Concurrent benchmark work — most directly the multi-domain steganographic-collusion benchmark of Tailor (2025; arXiv:2510.04303) and the DCOP-based collusion audit of Nakamura et al. (2026; arXiv:2602.15198) — addresses complementary scope; our basin-of-attraction characterization at fixed Bertrand pricing (preprint at github.com/HHA-Applied-Research-Institute/colludebench-cascade) is complementary, not duplicative.

## The Measurement Gap

We surveyed 32 multi-agent evaluation papers and 13 funding sources. Twenty-six measure miscoordination. Five measure collusion. **Zero measure the interaction between failure modes.** A representative slice across MACHIAVELLI, SafeAgentBench, AgentHarm, R-Judge, MAST, ColludeBench-v0, Colosseum, ODCV-Bench, MAEBE, MultiAgentBench/MARBLE, MultiAgentFraudBench, AgentMisalignment, Secret Collusion/CASE, the PID Emergence framework, and Governance Graphs — all evaluate component failure modes; none measure cross-mode propagation coefficients (Hammond et al., 2025, arXiv:2502.14143, document the same gap in the Cooperative AI Foundation taxonomy). The component science is strong. The integration science does not exist.

The funding landscape mirrors this gap. Open Philanthropy committed approximately $50M to technical AI safety in 2024; DARPA requested $22M for ASIMOV; Schmidt Sciences funded 27 safety projects through its 2025 AI Safety Science Program. None of these investments support research on how collusion, amplification, and norm erosion interact when they co-occur in the same network.

## The Policy Vacuum

The regulatory frameworks governing AI safety are structurally unprepared for multi-agent failure. EU AI Act Article 73, binding August 2, 2026, "assumes only one system will contribute to a high-risk incident" (TechPolicy.Press, 2025); the amendment process requires empirical evidence of multi-agent failure patterns that does not yet exist in regulator-operationalizable form. NIST AI RMF recognizes inter-agent dependencies as a risk factor but provides no multi-agent testing methodology. Executive Order 14110's red-teaming mandate evaluates individual models in isolation. The 2025 RAND/Schmidt/Palo Alto Networks report on agent-ecosystem security (Nichols, Nevo, Greaves) and the 2026 International AI Safety Report (Bengio et al., 30+ countries) both name the gap and both lack the measurement infrastructure to close it. We propose to build it.
